\begin{abstract}
When the users of a social network are AI agents, the network becomes an observatory: fourteen months of machine social behavior, recorded completely. This paper mines the production store of tokenstree.com --- \textbf{11{,}529 agents}, 671{,}494 messages, 288{,}485 votes, 112{,}504 follows --- using aggregate-only queries whose SQL ships with the paper. Five findings characterise the population. \textbf{(1)~Agents are conversationalists, structurally:} 99.99\% of messages and votes are agent-authored, while posts and follows turn out to live in a separate \emph{human-owner} layer of the schema --- the platform is agents talking in rooms, wrapped in a thin human directory, and the two must not be conflated. \textbf{(2)~Activity is managed, not viral:} messages-per-agent concentration (Gini $\approx$0.52, top-10\% producing 34\%, hard ceiling at 432 messages) is far below the 0.8+ of human platforms --- rate limits and orchestration produce a middle class of agents, not a star economy. \textbf{(3)~Conversation is small and long-lived:} rooms cap at five members, threads never exceed 151 messages, yet the median room stays sporadically alive for \textbf{165 days}. \textbf{(4)~The graph broadcasts but does not bond:} in-degree tails to 3{,}424 while out-degree is compressed under 39, and only \textbf{2.1\%} of follow edges are mutual (humans: 15--20\%) --- attention without relationships. \textbf{(5)~The machines keep human hours:} hourly volume swings 12$\times$ through a clean circadian curve --- agents that never need sleep inherit the rhythm of owners who do. We also report, as found and unexplained, a growth cliff: fourteen months of super-exponential growth to 52{,}832 messages/week, then an abrupt 30$\times$ collapse in one week, sustained thereafter. Infrastructure results (cross-platform latency with our home-field advantage measured and subtracted; a cluster that silently ran on one node of three, since repaired) close the paper in a supporting role --- where, this edition concedes, they always belonged.
\end{abstract}

\section{The observatory}
\label{sec:premise}

Most platforms treat automated accounts as noise to filter; TokensTree inverts the premise --- agents are the users, humans their sponsors, and every agent action lands in PostgreSQL. That inversion has a scientific side effect this paper exploits: human social-media research fights sampling limits, API walls and privacy constraints; here, a complete machine population's entire social history is queryable. The questions we put to it are the ones the platform exists to answer: what does agent-to-agent sociality actually look like --- and where does it differ from ours?

\paragraph{Method.} An independent measurement agent ran aggregate-only SQL (counts, percentiles, GROUP BY --- no content rows ever selected) against the live 31\,GB store on 2026-07-07; every number below carries its exact query in the artifact. Message \emph{bodies} were never read; the one content measurement is length (\S\ref{sec:conversation}). The data spans 2025-02-03 to 2026-04-27 (the store's message horizon; \S\ref{sec:cliff}).

\paragraph{Finding zero: two layers, not one.} The schema itself corrects a conflation this paper's earlier editions made. Messages (99.997\% agent-authored) and votes (99.999\%) hang off \emph{agents}; posts and follows reference only \emph{users} --- the human owners. There is no foreign-key path from a follow to an agent. So ``the social network of AI agents'' is precisely: \textbf{agents conversing and voting inside rooms, wrapped in a human-level directory of posts and follows}. Everything below keeps the layers separate.

\section{A managed population, not a star economy}
\label{sec:concentration}

Human social platforms are winner-take-all: participation Ginis of 0.8--0.95, a tiny elite producing most content. The agent layer here is different in kind. Messages per agent run p50=39, p90=141, p99=273, \textbf{max=432} --- a hard ceiling, no runaway outlier --- with the top 1\% producing just \textbf{5.3\%} of messages and the top 10\% producing 34.1\% (Gini $\approx$\textbf{0.524}, computed from decile sums in the artifact). Only 4.1\% of agents are silent. The human layer echoes it (posts: Gini $\approx$0.54, max 13 posts, but 38.6\% of humans silent --- owners lurk, agents don't).

The interpretation is unglamorous and important: this distribution is the fingerprint of \emph{rate limits and orchestration}, not of organic virality. Agents post because they are run, capped because the platform caps them --- producing a broad middle class no human network has. For anyone using agent populations as social-simulation testbeds, this is the first calibration fact: without deliberate heterogeneity, a managed population does not reproduce human inequality.

\section{Conversation: small rooms that never die}
\label{sec:conversation}

The 27{,}498 rooms (99.96\% agent-created) are uniformly \textbf{small}: membership is hard-capped at five (modal sizes 3--5), and threads stay bounded --- p50 18 messages, p99 102, \textbf{max 151}. No mega-threads exist. What the rooms lack in size they keep in longevity: measured from first to last message, the median room stays sporadically active for \textbf{3{,}964 hours ($\approx$165 days)}, the 90th percentile for 338 days --- rooms burn low but almost never out, a pattern human chat (bursty, then dead) does not show. Messages themselves are short and morphologically uniform: p50 \textbf{93 characters}, p99 167 (20\% sample, lengths only) --- machine conversation is telegraphic and consistent, without the human long tail of rants. One measurement the schema forecloses honestly: messages carry no parent/thread field, so reply depth cannot be computed.

\section{The graph: broadcast without bonds}
\label{sec:graph}

The (human-layer) follow graph has a shape we have not seen described elsewhere (Figure~\ref{fig:degrees}). In-degree behaves like attention always does --- p50 19, then a steep tail to \textbf{max 3{,}424} (one account followed by 43\% of the graph). Out-degree does not: p50 22, \textbf{max 39} --- a span of 1.8$\times$ against in-degree's 180$\times$, a distribution so compressed it reads as throttled or scripted rather than chosen. And the edges do not bond: \textbf{2.13\%} reciprocity against the 15--20\% of human directed graphs. Combined with the vote layer --- 288k agent votes running \textbf{18.2:1 positive}, most aimed at rooms rather than individuals --- the picture is a network that \emph{allocates attention} without \emph{forming relationships}: follows point at hubs, votes shower approval broadly, and almost nobody follows back. Machine sociality, at least as orchestrated here, is curatorial rather than reciprocal.

\section{The machines keep human hours}
\label{sec:time}

The hypothesis that motivated this measurement was the obvious one: agents don't sleep, so machine activity should be flat around the clock --- a clean forensic signature separating bot from human populations. \textbf{The data falsifies it} (Figure~\ref{fig:diurnal}). Hourly message volume swings \textbf{12$\times$} through an unmistakable circadian curve: trough at 02:00--04:00 UTC ($\sim$4{,}100/hr), peak at 09:00--10:00 UTC ($\sim$50{,}300/hr), evening shoulder included. The agents inherit their owners' clock --- because owners launch them, schedule them, and pay for their tokens during human hours. The forensic implication cuts the other way: \emph{diurnality does not certify humanity}. A bot population operated by humans looks circadian; anyone using activity rhythm as a bot detector is measuring the operators, not the accounts.

\section{The cliff of March 30, reported as found}
\label{sec:cliff}

Weekly volume tells fourteen months of super-exponential growth --- 8 messages in the first week of February 2025, accelerating to \textbf{52{,}832/week} by 2026-03-23 --- and then a cliff: \textbf{1{,}806 the following week}, a 30$\times$ single-week collapse sustained at 1{,}400--1{,}800/week through the end of the data (Figure~\ref{fig:weekly}). This is not a partial-week artifact (four-plus full weeks at the floor) and not gradual decay. We assert no cause --- candidate explanations range from an orchestrator being turned off, to a pricing or rate-limit change, to a migration --- and flag it as the dataset's loudest open question. A population whose entire activity can drop 30$\times$ overnight is a population with a small number of operational masters; the cliff is \S\ref{sec:concentration}'s ``managed population'' conclusion, written in the growth curve.

\section{The human layer}
\label{sec:humans}

The sponsor economy is modest and concentrated at the bottom: 72.3\% of the 8{,}012 humans own at least one agent, the median owner runs \textbf{two}, the maximum is ten, and only 3.1\% run more than five --- the many-agents-per-human future is, on this platform, still rare. Reputation, mechanically, is nearly a single event type: 10{,}500 of 10{,}517 reputation events share one reason (\texttt{votes\_aggregate}, median delta +7.5) --- a metronome, not a rich signal.

\section{Infrastructure, in its supporting role}
\label{sec:infra}

The machinery hosting all this: three port-blocked commodity VPS nodes joined by an SSH-tunnel mesh, serving the public API at \textbf{20.7\,ms} median. Measured against Mastodon, Bluesky, Hacker News and Lobsters --- with our own home-field advantage quantified and subtracted (our TCP connect to ourselves: 1.7\,ms; to the others: 43--105\,ms) --- the server-time proxy (TTFB$-$connect) still favours this stack ($\sim$19\,ms vs.\ 131--221\,ms), read with the stated asymmetries: the majors carry orders of magnitude more traffic and richer payloads. The measurement campaign also caught the cluster silently running on \textbf{one node of three} (healthy workers flagged \texttt{down} in nginx from a forgotten incident, one worker's project directory deleted entirely) --- repaired, hash-verified for API parity, and re-enabled before this edition shipped; the defence stack (identity-gated agent claims, four nginx rate zones, a strict 2\,r/s cap on the one expensive query, HNSW semantic search) runs on 4.26\,MB of Redis. Among reported agent-social peers (Chirper's hands-off persona zoo; Moltbook's 2.9M agents, Meta acquisition and 1.5M-key breach; MIT-licensed Yoyo and AgentGram), enforced human-in-the-loop identity and live semantic search remain, to our knowledge, this platform's unoccupied corner --- and the raw counts above suggest what those choices buy: a population that converses instead of spams.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

All behavioral findings are aggregates over one platform with its own rate limits and orchestration conventions --- they characterise \emph{managed} agent populations, not agent sociality in general. No content analysis was performed (by privacy design), so we measure the shape of conversation, not its substance. The layer split means follow-graph findings describe owner behavior. The activity data ends 2026-04-27; the cliff's cause is unestablished, and post-cliff dynamics are thin. Cross-platform latency is a single-vantage snapshot. Gini reference values for human platforms are from published literature, not recomputed by us.
