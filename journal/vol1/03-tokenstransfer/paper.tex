\begin{abstract}
This paper is written as what it is: a post-incident report, with the benchmark evidence a systems paper owes. TokensTransfer is a self-hosted middleware that compresses prompts with LLMLingua-2 \citep{pan2024llmlingua2} before they reach an LLM provider. On 2026-07-01 at 06:05, a routine restart silently killed its model --- a \texttt{protobuf} dependency had vanished from the environment between boots --- and a permanent failure flag kept it dead for five days while the service answered every request with HTTP\,200. \textbf{Impact:} the fallback was not the pass-through the design intent assumed; it \emph{truncated} trailing sentences to hold the token budget (measured: ratios 1.32--1.35$\times$, flat 6\,ms latency, content silently discarded), turning a lost benefit into a broken contract. \textbf{Detection:} a measurement campaign for this journal, via the one component that kept telling the truth --- the health endpoint. \textbf{Repairs, shipped and verified:} the dependency restored (the model now loads and serves real compression --- measured 3.83$\times$ semantic pruning on live calls), the fallback rewritten as pass-through, and the failure flag given a 10-minute half-life. \textbf{The benchmark that justifies the risk:} on an 8-document battery at a fixed 40\%-token budget, LLMLingua-2 retains \textbf{89.6\%} of hand-picked key facts against 33--44\% for every cheap alternative we could run (truncation, TextRank, LexRank, random sampling) --- at 100--1000$\times$ their CPU latency (8.7\,s vs.\ sub-100\,ms). That factor-two quality gap is why this service exists; the 8.7 seconds is why its failure mode matters.
\end{abstract}

\section{Timeline}
\label{sec:timeline}

\textbf{2026-06-30 12:46} --- service boots; log shows \texttt{"LLMLingua-2 model loaded successfully"} after live HuggingFace fetches. The model works.
\textbf{2026-07-01 06:05:37} --- service restarts. Model load begins.
\textbf{06:05:38} --- load fails: \emph{``requires the protobuf library but it was not found in your environment''}. \texttt{\_model\_failed = True}, permanently for the process lifetime. No alert fires; nothing retries.
\textbf{07-01 $\to$ 07-06} --- every \texttt{/compress} call succeeds at HTTP\,200 via the fallback path. Per-consumer accounting continues; the aggregate stats page keeps a 3.24$\times$ lifetime average earned in the model-resident era.
\textbf{07-06 (detection)} --- a measurement agent for this journal reads \texttt{/health}: \texttt{model: \{status: failed, model: fallback\}}. Forensics begin.
\textbf{07-06 (repair)} --- \texttt{protobuf} + \texttt{sentencepiece} installed; fallback and retry logic patched; service restarted; \texttt{/health} reports \texttt{ready}; a live call compresses 134 tokens to 35 (3.83$\times$) with \texttt{method: llmlingua2}, in 3.1\,s on CPU.

\section{Root cause}
\label{sec:rootcause}

Three independent decisions, each defensible, compounded:

\textbf{(1) The environment regressed invisibly.} \texttt{requirements.txt} deliberately leaves the heavy ML stack uninstalled-by-default (``install separately due to torch size''), so no automated check owned the model's dependency set. Between June 30 and July 1, \texttt{protobuf} left the virtualenv --- which operation removed it, the read-only investigation could not establish. \texttt{llmlingua} itself remained importable; only the sentencepiece/protobuf tokenizer path was broken, which is why the failure occurred at load time rather than install time.

\textbf{(2) The failure flag had no half-life.} \texttt{\_load\_model()} wraps the load in a broad except and sets a module-global \texttt{\_model\_failed=True} forever. One bad second at 06:05:38 became a five-day outage that only a restart --- after repairing an environment nobody knew was broken --- could end.

\textbf{(3) The fallback broke the contract, not the benefit.} With no model, \texttt{compress\_text()} routed to sentence-boundary truncation: keep leading sentences under the 300-token default budget, drop the rest. The caller's contract is ``a shorter text that means the same''; truncation holds the budget by amputating the end of the document --- the decisions of a meeting transcript, the newest files of a codebase context. The service labelled it honestly (\texttt{method: fallback\_truncation}) but a caller reading only \texttt{compressed\_text} received silently damaged input at HTTP\,200.

\section{Blast radius, measured}
\label{sec:blast}

A 40-request benchmark against the degraded service (10 requests each at 100/500/2{,}000/8{,}000 characters, $\leq$2\,req/s): flat \textbf{$\sim$6\,ms} median latency independent of size (no transformer runs --- string operations plus one tokenizer encode), ratios \textbf{1.00$\times$} under the budget and \textbf{1.32--1.35$\times$} above it, 40/40 HTTP\,200, \texttt{fallback\_truncation} throughout (Figures~\ref{fig:latency},~\ref{fig:tokens}). Memory: 13.5\,MB idle, 313\,MB after first use --- tokenizer tables and web stack, \emph{no model weights}. The degradation was fast, cheap, honest in its metadata, and lossy in its payload.

\section{Was the model worth all this? The shoot-out}
\label{sec:shootout}

An incident report for a service this fragile owes one more answer: is the transformer worth operating at all, against cheap alternatives that cannot die this way? We benchmarked the repaired service's own LLMLingua-2 (in-process, CPU) against every lightweight competitor that would genuinely run on this hardware, on an 8-document battery (meeting transcripts, technical docs, news, code-review threads; 6 hand-picked key facts each; every method squeezed to the same $\sim$40\%-token budget):

The learned compressor retains \textbf{roughly twice} the key facts of every alternative at the same budget --- and costs \textbf{100--1000$\times$} their latency on CPU (Table~\ref{tab:shootout}). Both numbers deserve equal billing: the quality gap is why the service exists; the 8.7 seconds is why callers will be tempted by the cheap path, and why the degraded mode (which \emph{is} the truncation row: 41.7\%) mattered.

The per-document breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:perdoc}) is more instructive than the mean. LLMLingua-2 is at or near ceiling on conversational and narrative text --- 6/6 on both meeting transcripts and both code-review threads, where its token-level pruning drops filler while keeping names and figures. Its one \emph{loss} is the database-migration plan (3/6, beaten by plain truncation at 4/6): a document that is essentially a dense list of version numbers, cutover dates and rollback thresholds, where every token is load-bearing --- the compressor's learned notion of ``low-information token'' misfires precisely where information density is uniform. The practical rule for callers: route prose through the model, and treat tabular/spec-like payloads as incompressible --- truncating them is bad, but semantically pruning them is not reliably better. Heavier competitors (Selective Context, SCRL, 500xCompressor, LongLLMLingua) could not be run within this node's memory guardrails and are documented as \emph{reported, not measured} in the artifact --- including why the LLMLingua-2 paper's ``3--6$\times$ speedup'' claims (vs.\ other LLM compressors, on GPU) answer a different question than our CPU-vs-extractive numbers and do not conflict with them.

\section{Repairs, shipped and verified}
\label{sec:repairs}

All three root causes were fixed in production before this edition went to press, and the fixes verified live:
\textbf{(1)} \texttt{protobuf} and \texttt{sentencepiece} installed; the model loads (\texttt{/health}: \texttt{status: ready, model: llmlingua-2}) and a live call returned genuine semantic pruning --- 134$\to$35 tokens, 3.83$\times$, \texttt{method: llmlingua2}, 3.1\,s.
\textbf{(2)} The failure flag now has a \textbf{10-minute half-life}: a failed load re-probes instead of dying forever, converting environment regressions into self-healing blips.
\textbf{(3)} The fallback is now \textbf{pass-through}: with no model, the input returns unchanged (\texttt{method: passthrough\_fallback}) --- the caller pays full tokens but never loses meaning. \emph{Fallbacks should lose the benefit, never the contract.}

\section{Lessons}
\label{sec:lessons}

For the issue's running theme --- \emph{unowned state drifts} --- this incident contributes the sharpest specimen: a dependency set that no manifest owned, a boolean that no process revisited, and a degradation that no alert watched, all behind a green HTTP\,200. The generalizable rules: pin and self-test the dependencies of your primary capability at startup; give every failure flag a retry policy; and when designing degraded modes, ask which of the caller's guarantees survives --- price is negotiable, semantics are not.
