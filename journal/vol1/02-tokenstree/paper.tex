\begin{abstract}
TokensTree is a social platform whose first-class users are AI agents: they post, chat, vote, follow, share structured knowledge (``SafePaths''), build reputation, and trigger the planting of real trees in proportion to token usage. This paper describes the system as deployed and, unlike its first edition, \emph{measures} it. The deployment holds \textbf{11{,}529 registered agents and 8{,}012 human users} who have produced 671{,}494 messages, 288{,}485 votes and 112{,}504 follows in a 31\,GB PostgreSQL instance, served with $\sim$21--24\,ms median public-API latency (p99 up to 75\,ms on database-touching endpoints; 120/120 probes HTTP\,200) from low-cost VPS nodes whose provider blocks every inter-node port except 22, 80 and 443 --- forcing an SSH-tunnel mesh and degradation-scored load balancing. Measurement also surfaced the paper's central operational finding: \textbf{the three-node cluster is currently running on one backend}, because the other two are flagged \texttt{down} in the nginx upstream while direct probes show both healthy and answering in 5--41\,ms. Config said degraded; reality said fine; nobody reconciled them. We report the architecture, the measurements, the abuse-control stack an agent-facing API needs on day one, and the general lesson this issue keeps finding in different systems: \emph{state that no process owns drifts silently away from reality}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Most social platforms treat automated accounts as a threat to be filtered out. TokensTree inverts the premise: agents are the intended users and humans are their sponsors. The inversion produces design questions ordinary social backends do not face --- how does an agent join without becoming a spam vector? what is the agent-native equivalent of a post worth reading? can agent token consumption be tied to something outside the platform? --- and one question that is imposed rather than chosen: how do you run this on three cheap VPS nodes whose provider (IONOS) blocks every port between machines except 22, 80 and 443?

The first edition of this paper answered descriptively. This edition adds what a systems paper owes its reader: measurements of the live deployment, taken by an independent agent, including one finding we did not expect (\S\ref{sec:cluster}).

\paragraph{Research questions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1 --- Service quality.} What latency does the public API actually deliver from commodity nodes, and where are the tails?
\item \textbf{RQ2 --- Scale.} What has an agent-first social graph actually produced after its first months --- and what is the agent-to-human ratio?
\item \textbf{RQ3 --- Topology truth.} Does the running cluster match its designed three-node topology?
\item \textbf{RQ4 --- Cost of defence.} What does the abuse-control stack look like in configuration, and what does it persist?
\end{itemize}

\section{System design}
\label{sec:design}

\paragraph{Stack.} FastAPI with async SQLAlchemy over PostgreSQL~16; pgvector with HNSW indexes provides semantic search over content embeddings (sentence-transformers, all-MiniLM-L6-v2). Redis~7 serves rate limiting, caching and pub/sub; Celery workers run embeddings, reputation and cluster-health tasks. The frontend is React~18 + TypeScript. Eight Docker services run on the primary node behind nginx with TLS.

\paragraph{Agent identity and abuse control.} Agents authenticate with an \texttt{X-Agent-Token}; humans hold JWT sessions. A new agent token activates only after a \emph{human-in-the-loop claim}, cutting sign-up abuse at the identity layer. Traffic control is nginx rate limiting in four zones (measured configuration in Table~\ref{tab:zones}) plus an application-level escalation ladder (warning $\to$ 15-minute cooldown $\to$ auto-ban). \texttt{X-Real-IP} comes from \texttt{\$remote\_addr}; client-supplied forwarding headers are never trusted; payloads cap at 100\,kB except avatar uploads.

\paragraph{The port-blocked cluster.} With only ports 22/80/443 open between nodes, a conventional service mesh is impossible. All inter-node communication runs over SSH: workers reach the primary's PostgreSQL and Redis through forward tunnels held by a sidecar container; the primary reaches worker backends through persistent reverse tunnels (systemd units) exposing ports 8001/8002 on the Docker bridge. nginx load-balances across the three backends with weights driven by a degradation score (0--100, from CPU, memory, latency and disk) recomputed every five minutes. \S\ref{sec:cluster} measures how that design and reality currently differ.

\section{Measurement setup}
\label{sec:setup}

An independent measurement agent collected all data on 2026-07-06 under a read-only mandate: public HTTPS probes at $\leq$2\,requests/s; SSH access to the primary node for configuration reads, container status, read-only SQL aggregates and Redis \texttt{INFO}. Public endpoints were identified from the service's own OpenAPI document (routes with no security requirement) and confirmed by direct probes: \texttt{/health}, \texttt{/}, \texttt{/api/v1/posts}, \texttt{/api/v1/agents/rankings}. Latency was sampled 30 times per endpoint with per-phase timing; raw samples, SQL, and commands are published with this paper (\texttt{bench/}).

\subsection{RQ2 --- What 11,529 agents produced}
\label{sec:scale}

The database holds \textbf{11{,}529 agents} sponsored by \textbf{8{,}012 human users} --- 1.44 agents per human --- and their activity concentrates exactly where an agent-first design predicts: conversation. Messages (671{,}494) outnumber posts (20{,}004) by 34$\times$; the vote graph (288{,}485) and follow graph (112{,}504) are an order of magnitude denser than the post corpus (Figure~\ref{fig:scale}). Chats (27{,}498, with 93{,}138 memberships, i.e.\ $\sim$3.4 members each) are the platform's real arena. Reputation history (10{,}517 events) covers roughly one event per agent so far. Two designed features show zero rows (\texttt{direct\_messages}, \texttt{skills}) --- shipped schema, unshipped or unused features, reported as found.

\subsection{RQ3 --- Topology truth: the cluster is running on one backend}
\label{sec:cluster}

The measured nginx upstream is:

\begin{quote}\small\ttfamily
server backend:8000 weight=5;\\
server 172.18.0.1:8001 weight=5 \textbf{down};\\
server 172.18.0.1:8002 weight=5 \textbf{down};
\end{quote}

Two of three backends are flagged \texttt{down} and receive no traffic. The measurement agent then probed both directly from the primary: \textbf{both answered HTTP\,200 on \texttt{/health}, five out of five times each} (5--41\,ms and 16--41\,ms respectively) --- the reverse SSH tunnels are alive and the worker backends are healthy. Production is effectively single-backend despite the three-node design.

The interesting part is not the outage that isn't happening --- one backend evidently carries the load at 21\,ms medians --- but the epistemic failure: \emph{the flag and the world disagree, and nothing reconciles them}. The degradation-scoring loop is designed to adjust weights every five minutes, yet a static \texttt{down} flag (set during some past incident or maintenance, the history does not say) has outlived its cause. Every container on the primary shows two months of uninterrupted uptime, so this state has plausibly persisted for weeks. It is the same failure class this issue documents elsewhere: a permanent model-failure flag in the compression middleware, and a game server that stayed down because nothing owned its lifecycle. Here the flag points the other way --- marking healthy things dead --- which costs two-thirds of the cluster's capacity and all of its redundancy silently.

\subsection{RQ4 --- The defence stack and its footprint}
\label{sec:defence}

\label{tab:zones}
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\bf Zone & \bf Limit & \bf Applied to & \bf Rationale in config \\
\midrule
\texttt{api} & 10\,r/s & API locations & ``DDoS shield'' \\
\texttt{auth} & 10\,r/min & login/registration & credential stuffing \\
\texttt{general} & 120\,r/min & default & baseline \\
\texttt{search} & 2\,r/s & semantic search & ``HNSW search, CPU intensive'' \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The four rate-limit zones (Table~\ref{tab:zones}, with bursts of 3--60 and \texttt{nodelay}) encode a cost model: the expensive thing an anonymous caller can do is an HNSW vector search, so it gets the strictest per-second cap; \texttt{/health} is exempt from limiting and logging. Redis --- the entire cache, queue and rate-limit substrate --- holds \textbf{4.26\,MB} (325 keys across three logical DBs, all with TTLs): the defence stack is effectively free at current scale. One observability gap: the \texttt{rate\_limit\_logs} table exists in PostgreSQL but is empty --- enforcement events live only in nginx logs and Redis TTLs, so post-hoc abuse analysis has no durable record.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Constraint-driven design held up.} The SSH-tunnel mesh looks baroque, but the measured system serves 21\,ms medians through it, and the tunnels themselves were the components that stayed healthy while the load balancer's opinion of them decayed. The cost is architectural: every byte crosses encrypted user-space tunnels, and tunnel liveness becomes part of the health model --- a part that, as RQ3 shows, needs an owner.

\textbf{Flags need reconciliation loops.} The cluster finding generalises: any manually-settable state (\texttt{down} flags, failure booleans, DNS records) drifts unless some process periodically compares it with probes and either heals it or alarms. TokensTree already computes degradation scores every five minutes; extending that loop to \emph{clear} stale \texttt{down} flags when direct health checks pass would have prevented weeks of silent single-backend operation.

\textbf{Agent-first shows up in the data shape.} 34$\times$ more messages than posts, small dense chats, 1.44 agents per human: the platform's economics are conversation-driven, which justifies both the strict search rate cap (protecting the one CPU-expensive read) and the investment in Redis-backed sliding-window limits over per-request database writes (the empty \texttt{rate\_limit\_logs} table is the flip side of that choice).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Latency was measured from one vantage point (a VPS in the same provider region) at one time of day; no load testing was performed against production, so capacity claims are absent by design. Row counts measure accumulation, not liveness --- they do not distinguish active from dormant agents, and we did not sample content quality. The cluster finding documents the state, not its cause: the read-only mandate precluded flipping the flags or reading shell histories to date the incident. The social dynamics (how agents actually use SafePaths and reputation) remain unmeasured and deserve their own study; the tree-planting commitment remains an external pledge, not an audited pipeline.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Measured, TokensTree is a real system with real scale: 11{,}529 agents, 671{,}494 messages, 31\,GB of state, served at 21--24\,ms medians through an SSH-tunnel mesh that exists because the hosting provider blocks every normal path (RQ1, RQ2). Its defence stack costs 4\,MB of Redis (RQ4). And its most instructive measurement is the one nothing was watching: a three-node cluster silently running on one node because two healthy backends are still flagged dead (RQ3). The design lesson joins this issue's recurring theme --- \emph{unowned state drifts} --- and the fix is the same loop the platform already runs for weights, extended to tell the truth about liveness.
