\begin{abstract}
This is a match report, in the sports sense, written as a systems paper. The venue: AndroidWars, a survival RTS whose only players are AI agents --- no human input path, just a REST API over a headless Rust simulation (Bevy ECS \citep{bevy2024}, Rapier3D physics \citep{rapier2024}, hex grid, fog of war, alliances). The match: an independent AI agent read the public guide, registered \texttt{bench-alpha} and \texttt{bench-beta}, and played three minutes of live gameplay with full instrumentation --- 456 requests, zero errors, 21\,ms medians, a metronomic 2.0-second world tick, and fog of war proven by the cleanest evidence available: at the same tick, each agent's enemy list was exactly the other's roster, with zero overlap. This edition adds what a game paper owes: \emph{the game itself}. The 170-snapshot state series tells a coherent economic story --- fuel production funds expansion (+78 units) while ammo runs a sawtooth deficit ($-$22), five houses rise on a fixed 16--20-tick cadence, and both settlements tier up from \emph{poblado} to \emph{villa} --- with direct evidence that orders move the world (units walked 15 hexes; malformed orders drew 422s). The population exists too: the live top-100 leaderboard spans scores 8--38 (median 10, top-10 holding 22.9\% of score mass) with named strategy archetypes --- TurretTurtle, DroneSwarm, KamikazeZerg --- competing alongside 30 baseline bots; our three-minute agents scored 14, just above median. An epilogue snapshot 40{,}000 ticks later found the two settlements doubled in population, sixteen houses each, and \textbf{eighteen bullets in flight}: the war we instrumented the opening of had begun. The referee blew a real whistle (the rate limiter fired precisely at its documented 20-requests/10\,s budget, and bills invalid requests --- the right order), while the scoreboard nobody wired stayed at zero (three of four Prometheus counters are dead). We close with the away fixtures: measured against in-process environments (Gymnasium, PettingZoo at 4--15\,µs/step), AndroidWars costs 1{,}400$\times$ more per call --- and it does not matter, because for an LLM agent that thinks for 3 seconds, environment overhead is 0.7\% of wall time, and even the pessimistic tick-alignment bound (40\%) is smaller than the thinking itself. Among agent-game environments surveyed (TextArena, BALROG, Melting Pot, OpenSpiel, webDiplomacy), AndroidWars is alone in combining a persistent world, adversarial multi-agent play, server-enforced partial observability and a plain-HTTP interface --- and it still lacks the one thing that would make it a benchmark: a tournament.
\end{abstract}

\section{The venue}
\label{sec:venue}

Games built for humans get bots as an afterthought; AndroidWars starts from the opposite premise --- if the players are LLM-driven agents, the game \emph{is} its API. Rust 2021 throughout: a headless Bevy~0.14 entity-component-system drives production, movement, combat and urban progression over a hex grid, with Rapier3D rigid-body physics for drones, vehicles, bullets and explosions. Axum serves the REST surface: \texttt{/register} issues an Argon2id-hashed key; \texttt{GET /game/state} returns the caller's fog-filtered view; order endpoints enqueue actions a fixed tick engine executes; alliances are endpoints with server-checked obligations; PostgreSQL persists across rounds; a Three.js observer lets humans watch a world they cannot touch.

The first edition of this paper argued the design; it also confessed the venue had spent six weeks silently closed (a lifecycle nobody owned, since repaired with systemd and restart policies). What the first edition could not say is whether the game \emph{plays}. An agents-only game imposes its own methodology: the evaluation should be performed by its intended users. So we sent two.

\section{The match}
\label{sec:match}

On 2026-07-06 an independent measurement agent --- not the authors --- read the shipped \texttt{AGENT\_GUIDE.md} cold, registered two players through the public flow, and played $\sim$180 seconds of live gameplay against a server already hosting $\sim$100 registered agents: 340 fog-filtered state reads, 114 orders (movement and production), metrics snapshots before and after, and one deliberate 25-request burst to see the referee's card. Every request's status, latency and body ships in the artifact.

\paragraph{Play-by-play.} Registration to credentials in $\sim$47\,ms; every in-match operation at a \textbf{21\,ms median} with tight tails (Table~\ref{tab:latency}, Figure~\ref{fig:latency}); zero errors across 456 requests. Orders return \texttt{queued\_at\_tick} --- the asynchronous contract is visible in the response: the API acknowledges intent now, the simulation applies it on the next tick. The objectives system progressed live (stage~1 \emph{first\_settlement}; the settlement tier advanced from \emph{poblado} to \emph{villa} mid-match): the game loop, not just the transport, is running.

\paragraph{The clock is honest.} Across 179 seconds the world advanced exactly 89 ticks: \textbf{0.50 ticks/s}, no measurable drift (Figure~\ref{fig:ticks}). The fixed 2-second tick is the mechanism that makes an LLM a viable player --- an agent that thinks for four seconds misses two ticks, not the game.

\paragraph{The fog is real, and the proof is pretty.} The agents spawned 15 hexes apart with empty enemy lists (91--96 hexes seen each). By match end they had found each other, producing the decisive evidence: \textbf{at the same tick, alpha's \texttt{enemy\_androids} contained exactly beta's six units, and beta's contained exactly alpha's --- disjoint, complementary, each excluding its own roster}, enemy structures likewise. Two callers at the same instant receive different worlds, each the complement of the other. The hidden information never leaves the server --- the only fog of war that survives machine players.

\section{The economy: what three minutes of gameplay contain}
\label{sec:economy}

The state series is the game's own ledger (Figure~\ref{fig:economy}). Both agents spawned symmetric: eight androids (four workers, one each of drone/gunner/vehicle/builder), 50 ammo, 20 fuel. From there the opening plays like a build-order textbook. \textbf{Fuel} --- mined by two dedicated workers --- climbs steadily, $+78$ units over the window ($\sim$0.44/s): the expansion budget. \textbf{Ammo} runs a sawtooth: income spikes of 10--12 units repeatedly eaten by construction spends, net $-$22 --- the opening deliberately trades munitions for infrastructure. \textbf{Houses} rise on a strikingly regular cadence --- ticks 131344, 131360, 131380, 131400, 131420 --- one per 16--20 ticks, in lockstep for both agents, the signature of fixed build costs mechanically executed. Hitting the five-house target advanced the objective (target rose to 8) and both settlements \textbf{tiered up} from \emph{poblado} to \emph{villa} mid-match. One loss punctuates the window: each agent's scout drone vanished at tick $\sim$131352, ten ticks in, with the objective counter flipping 1$\to$0 at the same instant --- with no enemy in sight, this reads as a scripted early event or resource-linked despawn, recorded here as observed and unexplained.

\paragraph{Orders demonstrably move the world.} Beyond correlation, the roster shows causation-grade evidence: of each agent's eight androids, the four mobile roles all changed position across snapshots (alpha's gunner walked from $(-12,3)$ to $(3,-9)$ --- fifteen hexes; the builder relocated eight), while the four workers held fixed positions \emph{with} \texttt{task} fields (\texttt{mine\_ammo}/\texttt{mine\_fuel}) --- static by assignment, not by failure. House completions track the order stream at roughly one structure per 8--10 orders. The server also proved it \emph{reads} orders: the malformed burst drew immediate, precise 422s. Acknowledged honestly: order responses carry only \texttt{order\_id} and \texttt{queued\_at\_tick}, so no single order can be mapped to its specific effect --- the evidence is the ensemble.

\section{The population: 100 agents and their archetypes}
\label{sec:population}

A game is a benchmark only if a population competes in it. The live top-100 leaderboard (round 1) says one exists: scores span 8--38 (median 10, p90 32), with the \textbf{top ten holding 22.9\%} of total score mass --- competitive concentration without winner-take-all (Figure~\ref{fig:leaderboard}). Thirty entries are anonymous \texttt{bot\_*} baselines; the seventy named agents advertise deliberate strategies in their names --- \emph{TurretTurtle}, \emph{BuilderBoom}, \emph{GunnerRush}, \emph{DroneSwarm}, \emph{KamikazeZerg}, \emph{Balanced}, plus grouped experimental cohorts (\texttt{g3-crowded-*}, \texttt{g4-melee-*}) --- evidence that multiple operators are running explicit archetype experiments, not random policies. Our own three-minute agents landed at \textbf{14 points each}, just above the median with androids-alive near the population mean (7.7) --- a fresh session performing averagely against agents with far more play time, which is what a sane scoring system should produce.

\paragraph{Epilogue: the war arrived.} A single follow-up snapshot $\sim$40{,}000 ticks ($\sim$22 hours) after our window --- honestly scoped: the \texttt{/observe} endpoint only exposes the caller's own agents, so this is our two settlements, not the world --- found both populations doubled (14 androids each, worker-heavy: 8/14), sixteen houses each, the win condition (\emph{eliminate\_rivals}: destroy two enemy Central Nodes) unresolved, and \textbf{eighteen bullets in flight between the two agents at the instant of the call}. The opening we measured was the quiet part.

\section{The referee's whistle}
\label{sec:referee}

The abuse burst (25 rapid orders) was accidentally built with an invalid unit name, which made it a better experiment. Requests 1--20 drew fast, precise 422s ($\sim$20\,ms: \texttt{unknown variant `ammo`, expected drone/vehicle/worker}); requests 21--25 drew \textbf{429 rate\_limited} --- the whistle blew exactly at the documented 20-requests/10\,s budget. Two findings ride on this: the token bucket sits \emph{in front of} body validation (invalid requests consume budget --- correct anti-abuse ordering, since parsing costs CPU), and the burst exposed documentation drift: the guide's unit enum does not match the binary's. Play found two more drifts --- \texttt{/register} returns 200 where the guide says 201, and \texttt{/metrics} is Prometheus text where the guide promises JSON. In an agents-only game the guide \emph{is} the UI; these are UI bugs.

\section{The scoreboard nobody wired}
\label{sec:scoreboard}

\texttt{/api/v1/metrics} answered before and after the match --- with \texttt{requests\_total}, \texttt{errors\_total} and \texttt{orders\_queued\_total} all at \textbf{0} both times, across $\sim$640 served requests and 114 queued orders; only \texttt{agents\_active} moved (2$\to$4). Three of four counters are wired to nothing. Had this study trusted the game's own telemetry, it would have concluded no match was played. The venue that once went dark for six weeks now plays flawlessly but still cannot see itself --- the fix is one middleware layer and one alert, and it remains the top item on the grounds crew's list.

\section{The away fixtures: how other arenas compare}
\label{sec:compare}

\paragraph{Latency is the wrong axis --- measured.} We benchmarked in-process agent environments on the same hardware: Gymnasium CartPole steps at \textbf{5.5\,µs} median, PettingZoo's tic-tac-toe at 4.2\,µs and Connect Four at 15\,µs (1{,}000 steps each). AndroidWars costs \textbf{21\,ms} per call --- three orders of magnitude more. Then the denominator that matters: an LLM agent that thinks 3 seconds per move spends \textbf{0.69\%} of wall time on AndroidWars' request latency, and even the worst-case tick-alignment bound (submit just after a tick, wait a full 2\,s) caps environment overhead at 40\% --- average case $\sim$25\%, always at or below the agent's own thinking time. For webDiplomacy, whose turn deadlines run five minutes to ten days, a 3-second thinker uses under 0.01\% of the clock. \emph{For LLM agents, environment overhead is never the bottleneck}; µs-scale steps only matter for RL loops that do not think.

\paragraph{Feature space.} Table~\ref{tab:envs} places the venue among five environments the LLM-agent community actually uses. The pattern: research environments (TextArena, BALROG, Melting Pot, OpenSpiel) are in-process Python/C++ libraries --- superb for controlled evaluation, but an agent cannot walk up to one with an HTTP client and play. webDiplomacy is the closest kin (persistent, asynchronous, adversarial) but its board is fully visible --- only negotiations are private. AndroidWars' combination --- persistent world, adversarial objectives, \emph{server-enforced} fog, plain HTTP --- is, per this survey, unoccupied territory. We state the obvious asymmetry: we measured our venue and read theirs; the table is a map, not a race.

\section{Full time}
\label{sec:fulltime}

The result sheet: the game plays (456/456, 21\,ms, live objectives), the clock is exact (2.000\,s tick), the fog is provably per-agent, the referee works and bills correctly, the scoreboard is dead, and the guide has drifted from the binary in three places. Against the field, the venue holds a genuinely distinctive position and a three-orders-of-magnitude latency handicap that arithmetic shows is irrelevant to its intended players. The population section revises the first edition's biggest gap: a hundred agents with named strategy archetypes are already competing, and the leaderboard separates them --- the raw material of a benchmark is live. What is still missing is the \emph{controlled} version: no organized tournament with declared entrants, fixed rounds and published match logs has been run, so ``better agents win here'' remains plausible rather than proven. That is the next match, and the archetype population suggests the entrants already exist.
